




# Civic Guild
A membership organization for technologists who believe democracy matters for the future of technology

## At a glance

*Purpose:* Organize technologists around shared democratic principles so technical expertise
can meaningfully support democratic governance of transformative technology.

*Who joins:* People in technical and policy roles across the U.S. tech ecosystem who commit to
the principles below and participate as individuals (not as representatives of their employers).

*What we do:* 1) lend technical talent to democracy-strengthening organizations, 2) identify opportunities for policy participation grounded in technical rigor, and 3) create a trusted community for mutual support.

## Why This Organization Exists

We are living through one of the most consequential technological transitions in human history.
Artificial intelligence and other transformative technologies are reshaping the fabric of daily life.
These tools hold extraordinary promise and extraordinary risk. At the same time,
evidence-based policymaking is increasingly politicized. International cooperation is fraying. And
the people closest to these technologies often have no organized way to bring their expertise
and values to bear on the democratic process.

Existing organizations working at the intersection of technology and policy tend to be think
tanks, advocacy groups, or industry lobbying operations. What is missing is a membership
community of technologists themselves organized around a shared commitment to democratic
governance, scientific rigor, and open society.

This organization fills that gap.

## The Principles That Unite Us

Members of this organization sign up for a shared set of foundational commitments about what
kind of society can manage transformative technology well:

1. *Democratic governance and the rule of law are essential for transformative
technologies to benefit humanity.* Advanced AI and other powerful technologies are
reshaping the distribution of power, knowledge, and opportunity faster than most institutions can
adapt. In moments of transformation this significant, societies need their governing systems to
be at their strongest - resilient, fair, transparent, and accountable. History shows that when rapid
change meets weak or chaotic governance, the result is not freedom, but consolidation of power
by those best positioned to exploit the disorder. The answer to a moment of extraordinary
disruption is not to abandon the structures of democratic governance but to reinforce them:
strong rule of law, independent institutions, and legitimate authority capable of making decisions
on behalf of the broad public interest.
1. *Rigorous scientific inquiry is essential for transformative technologies to benefit
humanity.* Our societies are being transformed by powerful new technologies in ways that are
not yet fully clear - affecting labor markets, information ecosystems, public health, democratic
participation, and countless other spheres. Without sustained, independent scientific research
grounded in empirical evidence, reproducible results, and intellectual honesty, we cannot see
these effects clearly enough to govern them wisely. Societies that allow ideology or political convenience to crowd out honest inquiry will not understand what is happening or formulate successful responses.
1. *Open societies that welcome talent and diverse perspectives are essential for
transformative technologies to benefit humanity.* America’s technological leadership has
been built by people from every part of the world. Immigrants and their children have been
indispensable to the development of AI and the industries surrounding it as researchers,
entrepreneurs, engineers, and institution-builders. A society that demonizes newcomers or
closes itself off from global talent not only commits a moral failure; it undermines the very engine
of innovation that gives it strength.
1. *International cooperation between democracies is essential for transformative
technologies to benefit humanity.* The development of AI and other transformative
technologies is a global enterprise, and its consequences do not respect borders. No single
nation can unilaterally ensure that these technologies are safe, equitable, and aligned with
democratic values. Protecting these goals requires sustained cooperation among democracies.
Democracies must lead in building these cooperative structures, not retreat into technological
nationalism that fragments governance and accelerates unsafe competition.

## Who We Are

This is a membership organization for people who work in technology companies in Silicon
Valley and the broader U.S. tech ecosystem. Membership is open to anyone who works in a
technical or policy role and commits to the principles above.
We are not a think tank, a lobbying firm, or an advocacy group. We are a community of
practitioners - people who build these technologies and understand, from the inside, what they
can do and what is at stake. Our credibility comes from our technical expertise. Our motivation
comes from our belief that democracy and the rule of law are necessary preconditions for it.
What We Do

### Community and Mutual Support

Working in technology while caring deeply about democratic values can be an isolating
experience. Some of our members may not have a space to regularly engage with these issues
or track the key developments affecting democracy. This organization provides a trusted
community: a place to share concerns, develop ideas, find solidarity, and build the relationships
that make collective action possible. We convene members through regular gatherings, working
groups, and private forums.

### Lending Technical Talent to Pro-Democracy Organizations

Democracy-focused organizations (e.g. civil society groups, newsrooms and independent
media, legal advocacy organizations) often lack the technical talent they need. At the same
time, technologists who want to contribute to democratic institutions rarely have a clear pathway
to do so. We bridge that gap by organizing and deploying our members’ skills where they are
needed most. This may include pro bono technical projects, embedded fellowships, volunteer
surge capacity during critical moments, and ongoing advisory relationships.

### Collaborative Policy Engagement

Technology policy is often shaped by people who do not fully understand the technology, or by
industry representatives whose obligations run to shareholders and investors rather than the
public. Our members bring a perspective that is technically informed, democratically committed,
and independent of any company’s interests. We collaborate to develop and deliver inputs to
relevant policy processes - including public comments, technical briefs, testimony, and direct
engagement with policymakers - grounded in our shared principles and collective technical
expertise.

## What Makes Us Different

*Practitioners, not observers.* Our members build these technologies. That gives us credibility
and insight that policy-focused organizations cannot replicate.

*Principles, not partisanship.* Our principles are about democratic governance, scientific rigor,
openness, and international cooperation — not about any political party or candidate. Members
across the political spectrum can and should find common ground here.

*A community, not an institution.* We are a membership organization, not a staff-driven think
tank. The energy, ideas, and action come from our members.

*Independent of industry.* While our members work at technology companies, the organization
is independent of any company. Members participate as individuals committed to shared
principles, not as representatives of their employers.
